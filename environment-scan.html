The environment scan

Three analyses at three levels — the wider economy, the industry, and the firm — that arrive at the same conclusion.

These appendix figures are the industry evidence behind the problem statement. On their own they are three separate scans; read together they point to the same conclusion from three directions. Each opens as a full, print-ready figure.

Three levels · one conclusion

All three point the same way: in this industry, your edge is your people — the expertise and relationships they hold. This research focuses on one lever the firm can pull: developing people's capability through real, on-the-job problems, in a way that also leaves a shared record the business can reuse. The agreement between three independent analyses is why that is worth investigating.

A1 The wider economy · Appendix 1 · Figure A1.1
PESTEL — the Australian context
A low-margin industry, exposed to trade shocks and skills shortages. The factor that matters most is people: skilled staff are scarce and easily poached in a tight labour market. Technology is an open gap — the industry has been slow to modernise, just as AI tools mature — which makes it realistic to capture knowledge cheaply, as a natural by-product of everyday work (IBISWorld 2025).

Porter's Five Forces — freight forwarding
As a smaller forwarder, the partner sits where the market pressure is greatest — small firms are squeezed by a few powerful shipping lines (which are muscling into forwarders' own work) and by larger rivals merging and buying up smaller ones. Firms this size can't win on price or scale, so they compete on the strength of their people — expertise, relationships and know-how — rather than cost (IBISWorld 2025).

 → A3 The firm · Appendix 3 · Figure A3.1
VRIO — the resource landscape
The partner's edge lives in its people — the hands-on expertise experienced staff build on the job, which is valuable, rare, and hard for rivals to copy (IBISWorld 2025). What this research investigates is how deliberately that expertise is developed and shared across the firm, especially as it grows by acquisition. The proposition: build professional development around real operational problems, so people grow their capability and, as a by-product, leave a record the business can reuse.

A4 The synthesis · Appendix 4 · Figure A4.1
SWOT — synthesis of the environment scan
The three scans consolidated into one 2×2: Strengths and Weaknesses from the firm layer (VRIO), Opportunities and Threats from the macro and industry layers (PESTEL · Five Forces). The signature move — the firm's experienced-staff know-how is a Strength and, because it isn't organised for capture, the same-named Weakness — sets up the WO hinge the TOWS matrix develops next (IBISWorld 2025).

 → A5 The strategy · Appendix 5 · Figure A5.1
TOWS — the worked matrix
The strategy layer over the SWOT: it pairs the quadrants into four moves (SO · ST · WO · WT) and shows they converge on one. SO and ST are the right plays but both depend on the capability being organised and retained; WT is the cost of skipping it. Only the WO cell acts on that — design capture and capability as one act. WO is the study (Markus 2001; IBISWorld 2025).
