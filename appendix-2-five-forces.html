Porter's Five Forces — freight forwarding

How the freight-forwarding industry is structured — read through Porter's five forces — and what each one means for a smaller, growth-by-acquisition firm like the partner.

Scope: Australian rail, air & sea freight forwarding (ANZSIC 5292); strength ratings follow IBISWorld's own assessment. Sources: IBISWorld 2025; IBISWorld 2026; Container News 2025; Mordor Intelligence 2026. All viewed 28 June 2026.

Threat of new entrants
Barriers · entry
Low

Little upfront capital is needed, so it is easy to set up — the real barriers are reputation, relationships and skilled staff, harder for small entrants without a recognised brand and “established training programs” (IBISWorld 2025, p. 38).

For the partnerWhat protects a firm here is its people — their skills and relationships — which can be lost when firms are acquired.

Power of suppliers
Strongest force
High

The shipping lines control space and pass volatile costs through; sea freight is dominated by a few players that are expanding into logistics themselves (IBISWorld 2025, 2026; Container News 2025).

For the partnerA smaller firm has little bargaining power here, so it competes on service and expertise, not price.

Core force
Competitive rivalry
Fragmentation · consolidation
Low

The market is split among many small firms (top four ≈29%; ~90% have under 20 staff), but larger firms that own more of the supply chain squeeze smaller ones on price, and mergers are common (IBISWorld 2025).

For the partnerSmaller firms get by on specialised service and reputation rather than size.

Power of buyers
Switching · rates
Moderate

With many firms to choose from and little cost to switching, customers hold the upper hand — tempered only by rate-adjustment clauses in contracts (IBISWorld 2025).

For the partnerStanding out on service makes customers less likely to switch away.

Threat of substitutes
Road · in-house · digital
Moderate

Road freight and (limited) in-house logistics, with online platforms that let customers bypass forwarders an emerging factor (IBISWorld 2025, 2026; Mordor Intelligence 2026).

For the partnerThe expert, people-led service is the part software can't easily or cheaply replace.

Structural verdict · the industry

A mature, fragmented, low-margin industry with one strong force working against small firms: a handful of powerful shipping lines that smaller players can't match on cost or scale. That leaves little room to compete on price — so small forwarders get by on specialised service and a reputation for expertise rather than price (IBISWorld 2025). Competing on the strength of their people is the realistic route the structure leaves open — which is why developing that capability is necessary, not optional.
