VRIO — the resource landscape

Three resources of a smaller, growth-by-acquisition forwarder, tested with VRIO — is each one Valuable, Rare, hard to Imitate, and Organised to make the most of? (Barney's framework.)

The firm is described at the level of its type, not from inside the partner; whether and how the partner is “organised” to develop and share capability is what the research investigates. Sources: IBISWorld 2025; Jobs and Skills Australia 2025; Industry Skills Australia 2025; recruiter salary guides (directional). All viewed 28 June 2026.

Resource 01
Global multi-office network

Valuable for reach and contacts (IBISWorld 2025), but only partly rare — almost every forwarder belongs to a network — and easy to copy. It is set up for moving freight, not for developing or sharing what people know.

ImplicationAbout level with rivals on reach — the channel capability could travel through, not the edge itself.

Resource 02
Customs-clearance expertise & ABF licensing

Customs clearance is licensed, specialist work that few small forwarders can do in-house (IBISWorld 2025): valuable, genuinely rare and hard to copy, given a shortage of licensed brokers and an ageing workforce (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025; Industry Skills Australia 2025). Whether it is organised — consistent and shared across a multi-office firm — is an open question.

ImplicationA lasting advantage where it is well organised; a scarce, regulated strength any strategy built on standing out would lean on.

Resource 03 · headline
The know-how and relationships of experienced staff

The hands-on know-how experienced freight and customs staff carry — how the work is really done, the regulatory detail, and the client relationships they hold. IBISWorld points to this as where value is mostly added — “value is primarily added through industry knowledge, contacts and purchasing transport space in bulk” (IBISWorld 2025, p. 37) — and it is rare and hard to copy because such staff are scarce, ageing and slow to replace (Jobs and Skills Australia 2025; Industry Skills Australia 2025). How deliberately a firm like the partner develops and shares that capability is the open question this research explores — with one risk worth examining: that growth by acquisition can add new teams faster than their know-how is passed on.

ImplicationIn VRIO terms, an advantage the firm holds but may not yet be fully set up to use — the “O” is what the research sets out to clarify.

Landscape hinge · the firm

So the picture is a capability the firm-type clearly holds but may not be fully set up to use — and the same economy-level scan shows why now: automation and AI are now good enough to support developing people through their everyday work, with a reusable record captured as a by-product — the opening. The industry rewards competing on people, and a firm like the partner holds that capability; how deliberately it is developed and shared is the open question this research sets out to answer.

The economy · PESTEL The industry · Five Forces The firm · VRIO → developing capability is within reach

Three levels, one conclusion: the wider economy, the industry and the firm all point the same way — in this industry the edge is your people, and developing their capability is the response that fits. This research explores how the partner develops that capability today, and where it could be strengthened. The case for acting is at once timely (the economy), necessary (the industry) and within reach (the firm).
